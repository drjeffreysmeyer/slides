Page 3 – Warm-Up: Matching Slopes to Points

Warm-Up – Matching Slopes to Points on a Curve

A smooth, S-shaped oscillating curve is drawn in blue on a white coordinate plane. Six filled dots are labeled:
  • A – lower-left region, on an upward slope, moderate incline.
  • B – just right of A, near a small local maximum, curve is flattening out (slope near zero or slightly negative).
  • C – near the bottom of a deep trough (local minimum), curve is nearly flat and about to turn upward.
  • D – just right of C, curve is rising steeply after the trough.
  • E – at the top of the tallest peak (global maximum in view), curve is flat — slope = 0.
  • F – just right of E, on a steep downward descent.
A two-column table to the right lists the slopes −3, −1, 0, 0.5, 1, and 2, each with a dropdown labeled “?” for students to fill in.

Problem: Match each labeled point to the given slope by reading the steepness and direction of the curve at each point.

Strategy:

  • At a local maximum or minimum, the tangent is horizontal: slope = 0.
  • Positive slope → curve rising left-to-right; negative slope → curve falling.
  • Steeper curve → larger absolute value of slope.

Page 4 – Ontology: Objects and Attributes

Ontology – Objects and Attributes

Sparse slide on a black background. The word “Attributes” appears in white text. Handwritten annotations in white include partial phrases: “Objects exist external to us” and fragments connecting objects to their attributes.

The course begins with a brief philosophical framing:

  • Objects exist externally to us in the world.
  • Objects possess attributes — properties or characteristics that can potentially be measured.
  • Mathematics gives us the language to describe and quantify those attributes.

Page 5 – Ontology: Quantities

Ontology – Quantities in Daily Life and Disciplines

Black slide with white handwritten text: “We encounter quantities in our day-to-day lives and in our disciplines.”

We encounter quantities constantly — temperature, speed, population, drug concentration, electrical current, and more. These quantities are the objects of study in differential equations.

A quantity is a measurable attribute of an object. When quantities change, we ask: how fast? relative to what? These questions lead naturally to derivatives and, therefore, to differential equations.

Page 6 – OAQ Activity

OAQ Activity – Objects, Attributes, Quantities

Black slide with the title “OAQ Activity” in white text. The body of the slide is largely blank, indicating this was an in-class interactive exercise.

In this in-class activity, students identify the three layers of a quantitative situation:

  • Object — the thing being studied (e.g., a patient, a fish population, a spring).
  • Attribute — a property of that object (e.g., the amount of drug, the number of fish, the displacement).
  • Quantity — a measurable, numerical version of the attribute (e.g., mass in mg, population count, meters from equilibrium).

Recognizing this structure is the first step in building a mathematical model.

Page 7 – Guided Exercise: Medicine in the Body

Guided Exercise – Medicine in the Body

White text on a black background presents the typed problem. Below, handwritten annotations in white chalk-style show the notation “y’(t) =” followed by an arrow and the equation “y = −ky for some k > 0”, indicating the instructor derived this live.

Scenario: A certain medicine is given to a patient. Once consumed, the body begins to metabolize the medicine and the mass undergoes decay in which the speed of decay is proportional to the mass in the body.

Variables:

  • \(t\) = number of hours since the medicine was taken (independent variable)
  • \(y = f(t)\) = mass of the drug remaining in the body, in milligrams (dependent variable)

Modeling the relationship: “Speed of decay is proportional to mass” means the rate of change of \(y\) is proportional to \(y\) itself, with a negative sign because mass is decreasing:

\[ y' = -ky \quad \mbox{ for some constant } k > 0 \]

Here \(k\) is the decay constant — a positive number that depends on the specific drug and the patient’s metabolism. The larger \(k\), the faster the drug is eliminated.

Type: First-order linear ODE. This is an example of exponential decay.

Page 8 – Guided Exercise: Logistic Population Growth

Guided Exercise – Logistic Population Growth

Black slide with white typed problem text at top. Below, handwritten notes in white develop the model in stages: “Above population N, population decreases”; “For small P, P ≈ kP”; “Want something [large] when P is small relative to N and [approaches zero] when P is close to N”. The logistic factor \((1 - P/N)\) is being built up collaboratively.

Scenario: A small population of fish might grow exponentially if the pond is large and food is abundant, but the growth rate will decline as the population increases and resources become scarce.

Variables:

  • \(t\) = number of days since the population was first observed
  • \(P = g(t)\) = population of fish (dependent variable)
  • \(N\) = carrying capacity (maximum sustainable population)
  • \(k\) = intrinsic growth rate constant, \(k > 0\)

Building the model step by step:

  1. For small \(P\): growth is approximately exponential, so \(P' \approx kP \).
  2. Above carrying capacity \(N\): population should decrease, so \(P' < 0\) when \(P > N\).
  3. We need a factor that equals 1 when \(P \approx 0\), equals 0 when \(P = N\), and is negative when \(P > N\). The factor \( (1- \frac{P}{N} )\) satisfies all three conditions.

The Logistic Equation:

\[ P' = k P \left(1- \frac{P}{N} \right) \]

This single equation captures both early exponential growth and the leveling off near the carrying capacity.

Key behavior: Solutions \(P(t)\) with \(0 < P(0) < N\) are S-shaped (sigmoidal), increasing toward \(N\) asymptotically.

Page 9 – Guided Exercise: Mass-Spring System

Guided Exercise – Mass-Spring System (Hooke’s Law)

Two side-by-side engineering diagrams on a white background:
  • Top diagram: A horizontal spring connects the left wall to a rectangular mass on the right. The mass is displaced to the right of the dashed vertical equilibrium line. A horizontal arrow labeled “Position \(x(t)\) at time \(t\)” shows the displacement from equilibrium.
  • Bottom diagram: The same setup but the mass sits exactly at the dashed equilibrium line, labeled “Position at rest.”
Both diagrams show the spring in a compressed/stretched state (top) vs. natural length (bottom).

Scenario: A mass attached to a spring (which is also attached to a wall) is stretched and released. It experiences a restorative force pulling it back toward equilibrium — this is Hooke’s Law.

Variables:

  • \(t\) = seconds since the mass was released
  • \(x = h(t)\) = displacement in meters from the equilibrium (rest) position
  • \(m\) = mass (kg), \(k\) = spring constant (N/m), \(k > 0\)

Hooke’s Law: The restoring force is proportional to displacement and directed opposite to displacement:

\[ F_{ ext{spring}} = -kx \]

Newton’s Second Law: \(F = ma\). Since acceleration is the second derivative of position, \(a = x''(t)\):

\[ mx'' = -kx \]

Equivalently:

\[ x'' + \frac{k}{m}x = 0 \]

This is a second-order linear ODE with constant coefficients. Its solutions are sinusoidal — describing perpetual oscillation.
